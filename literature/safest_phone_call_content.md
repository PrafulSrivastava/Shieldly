The safest phone call content is **not casual conversation**. The stronger evidence points toward an audible help-seeking and accountability message that does four things at once:

- names the situation clearly
- signals that other people now know
- gives a real location
- implies immediate contact or meet with others

# Resources

## Clear labeling beats ambiguity
In spectator research, explicit emergency wording drew more help than vague cues (Shotland & Stebbins, 1980). In adjacent refusal research, more definite verbal resistance was judged more effective than softer or ambiguous responses (Byers et al., 1987) (Yagil et al., 2006).

## Ambiguity can be misread
Work on men’s cue perception shows some men persistently misread friendliness or indirectness (Johnson et al., 1991). That makes casual chat a weak deterrent if the goal is to end pursuit.

## Street harassment often has a dominance and power element
That means a message that raises social risk for the pursuer may work better than one that merely sounds socially occupied 
(DelGreco et al., 2021) (Nitschke & Lam, 2021).

## There is some caution on direct confrontation
In harassment intervention work, direct strategies often stop behavior, but they are also the ones most tied to verbal or physical harm in some cases (Ford et al., 2024). So the safest call content may be **explicit without being provocative.**

# Conclusion
So the leading candidate message type is something like:

- an **overheard report to a real person** (or our real-time AI agent :D )
- calm, specific, and action oriented
- not insulting the follower
- not bluff heavy
- focused on location, movement, and imminent contact

Example shape, not final wording:

-“I’m on Elm and 3rd, a man is following me, stay on the line, I’m going into the pharmacy now.”

That seems better supported than:

- casual small talk
- indirect hints
- taunting or threatening the pursuer
- highly emotional but vague statements with no location or action cue

The main weak point in the evidence is that I do not yet see a paper directly comparing:

- casual phone conversation
- overheard explicit report of being followed
- direct address to the follower while on the phone
